\section{Summary, Conclusions, and Recommendations}

\subsection{Summary}

This study predicted Formula One race finishing tiers (Podium, Point Scorer,
No Points) using within-race telemetry data from the 2023 Bahrain, Saudi
Arabian, and Australian Grands Prix. A dataset of 2,880 lap-level
observations was constructed from FastF1 telemetry, with features engineered
to represent the physical dynamics of race strategy: tire age, a linear fuel
burn-off proxy, their mean-centered interaction term, and tire compound
dummies, with grid position as a baseline control.

Box's M test rejected the homogeneity of covariance matrices assumption
($p < 2.2 \times 10^{-16}$), ruling out discriminant analysis and
motivating multinomial logistic regression as the primary inferential model.
VIF diagnostics confirmed the absence of problematic multicollinearity (all
VIF $< 3.5$). The MLE model identified GridPosition ($p = 0.000$) and the
Degradation $\times$ Fuel interaction term ($p = 0.0095$ for Podium) as
statistically significant predictors. Leave-one-group-out cross-validation
across the three races yielded 62.36\% overall accuracy, with podium recall
reaching 89\%.

\subsection{Conclusions}

Based on the findings, the following conclusions are drawn:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{The null hypothesis is rejected.} At least one predictor---the
    Degradation $\times$ Fuel interaction term---has a statistically
    significant effect on the log-odds of finishing in the Podium tier
    ($p = 0.0095 < \alpha = 0.05$), after controlling for grid position.

  \item \textbf{Grid position is the dominant predictor.} Starting position
    is overwhelmingly significant for both the Podium ($p = 0.000$) and
    Point Scorer ($p = 0.000$) tiers, confirming that qualifying performance
    sets the baseline for race outcomes.

  \item \textbf{The tire--fuel interaction captures a distinct physical
    effect.} The significance of the Degradation $\times$ Fuel term for the
    Podium tier, even after grid position absorbs baseline car pace,
    demonstrates that the coupling of tire degradation and fuel burn-off is
    a measurable predictor of top-tier finishes---not merely noise.

  \item \textbf{The model generalizes across circuit types.} The LOGO-CV
    results, where the model was trained on two races and tested on a
    third, confirm that the statistical relationships hold across permanent,
    street, and parkland circuits.

  \item \textbf{Mid-field prediction remains challenging.} The lower recall
    for the Point Scorer tier (0.45) reflects genuine variance in mid-field
    racing that telemetry features alone cannot capture---DRS trains,
    undercut strategies, safety car timing, and team orders all introduce
    noise that is not represented in the current feature set.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Recommendations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Expand the race calendar.} Including all 23 races from a
    full F1 season would provide greater variance in weather conditions,
    circuit types, and team performance trajectories, strengthening both the
    statistical power and the generalizability of the model.

  \item \textbf{Incorporate weather variables.} Rain probability, track
    temperature, and ambient temperature directly affect tire degradation
    rates and compound selection. Including these would capture a significant
    source of within-race variance.

  \item \textbf{Model DRS and traffic effects.} Lap time delta to the car
    ahead, DRS activation history, and pit stop timing would help explain
    mid-field variance and improve Point Scorer tier predictions.

  \item \textbf{Explore non-linear tire degradation.} The current model
    treats tire age as a linear predictor, but degradation curves are known
    to be exponential after a ``cliff'' threshold. A piecewise or
    polynomial tire age term may better capture late-stint performance
    drops.

  \item \textbf{Compare with alternative classifiers.} Random forests,
    gradient-boosted trees, or ordinal logistic regression could be
    benchmarked against the multinomial logistic regression baseline to
    determine whether more flexible models improve classification
    performance.

  \item \textbf{Apply to other racing series.} The feature engineering
    framework---fuel proxy, tire degradation, interaction terms, grid
    position control---is transferable to any series with telemetry data
    (Formula 2, IndyCar, WEC) and could be validated as a general
    motorsport prediction methodology.
\end{enumerate}
